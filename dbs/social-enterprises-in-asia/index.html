SOCIAL ENTERPRISES The Businesses
of the future

Business that give back are on the rise—and we’re not
talking about big corporations with tangential CSR programs that look good on paper, but entire enterprises built specifically for doing good. In a radically-shifting world that is affected by climate change, social issues, and purpose-driven mindsets, social enterprises (SEs) are not a trend; they are
the business of the future.

Social Enterprises, Defined.

Let’s be clear, social enterprises are not traditional charities nor foundations; rather, they are sustainable businesses created for the purpose of contributing to society while generating income.

SEs are borne out of “creative solutions to society’s most pressing problems, designed to be just, affordable, and sustainable” (Phils, Dieglmeir and Miller 2008). Dynamic SEs are answerable to not just one, but two bottom lines: social good and profit.

Changing the World,
One Business Model At A Time.

Social enterprises are run by modern visionaries—entrepreneurs who recognize that making money and changing the world don't have to be mutually exclusive. They are transforming the economic landscape, proving that it is possible to build for-profit businesses around social and environmental missions.

Scaling Up Social Business

Social enterprises may start with good intentions, but as a relatively new, evolving concept with few benchmarks, the challenge lies in turning brilliant social innovations into viable businesses.

A.T. Kearney’s Social Enterprise Accelerator Model identifies three key factors that help social businesses scale up:

  • KEY FACTOR 1FOUNDATION
    ELEMENTS

    Includes articulating a clear vision and mission; identifying a core product or service that addresses the need of the target market; establishing “proof of concept” and methods of measuring impact; sourcing adequate funding; building a motivated team, and finding skilled and passionate leaders capable of navigating uncharted territories.

  • KEY FACTOR 2GROWTH LEVERS

    To boost the business include leveraging technology to created innovative, efficient options; building cost-effective platforms (delivery, production, infrastructure, support); and sharing knowledge through collaborative efforts with communities and fellow social entrepreneurs, as well as transparency with stakeholders.

  • KEY FACTOR 3Changed Attitudes
    and Behaviors

    Brought about by effective marketing efforts and information campaigns. Helping communities understand the significance and viability of social enterprises can influence policy makers, encourage government and public support, and pave the way for incentives and investments, ultimately establishing a sustainable social enterprise ecosystem.

The Rise of Social Enterprises in Asia

Social enterprises may have been on the economic radar since the 1980s, but it’s only in the past decade that the movement has really gathered steam around the globe.

In Asia, the continuing plight of developing nations (roughly two-thirds of the world’s poor) has called for creative innovations to address social issues, spurring the growth of local SEs. Neighboring first world Asian countries also have their own issues to contend with, such as ageing populations and waste management. With the help of financial institutions, philanthropic investors, and stakeholders, the Asian region is now taking greater ownership in solving its social and environmental issues.

Each country has its own unique ecosystem and nation-specific conditions—population, natural resources, culture, political landscape, and the business environment, etc—that dictate the type of social enterprises it needs. Here’s an overview of the social enterprise landscape in Asia, highlighting SEs supported by DBS.

ENTERPRISES IN ASIA

  • CHINA

    Heightened social awareness and ongoing economic reform has led to the growth in the innovation sector and the rise of SEs like Rice Donate, a mobile app connecting individuals, companies, and charities, based on their needs and demands. Operational leeway still poses a challenge for Chinese social entrepreneurs, but that hasn’t discouraged them from building enterprises that address poverty and education issues. Yi Bao Ji Hua provides quality, affordable insurance to protect the welfare of China’s low-income earners, while Better Education empowers youth with financial literacy for a better future.

  • HONG KONG

    Hong Kong benefits from greater funding and regulatory support for SEs. Its ageing population provided the impetus to create SEs such as Eldpathy, which increases awareness to cultivate compassion toward the elderly; Aesir, which harnesses virtual technology to help the special needs community cultivate life skills so they are not dependent on ageing parents. Meanwhile, the burgeoning coffee culture and ensuing waste concerns led to Eco-Greenergy, which kickstarted Hong Kong’s first ever coffee grounds recycling movement. And because Hong Kong is home to 6.4% ethnic minorities, WEDO Global uses multicultural education such as workshops and walking tours to embrace ethnic cultures for a more integrated, diverse, and loving society.

  • INDIA

    Millions of people living without access to electricity, clean water, shelter, healthcare, basic financing, and decent education has led to the emergence of numerous SEs focused on addressing these issues, such as Rovnost Healthcare (makes healthcare affordable and accessible to rural communities), Jeevtronics (designed an affordable, hand-cranked defribillator for clinics in undeveloped areas without electricity), Re-Materials (produces affordable, eco-friendly roofs to provide better shelter in Indian slums), and Zaya Learning Labs (provides holistic learning models for low-income children). As India is also the third biggest producer of waste worldwide, Sampurn(e)arth focuses on waste management, as well as transforming it into resources like organic fertilizer and cooking gas, while Greensole refurbishes shoes destined for landfills into new footwear for underprivileged children.

  • SINGAPORE

    The world’s most business-friendly regulatory environment (World Bank, 2013) paves the way for Singapores SEs, majority of which provide a service or business support, as well as offer solutions to lifestyle, social, trade and educational concerns. Boxgreen curates and delivers healthy, natural, portion-controlled snacks to your doorstep to promote a healthier society; Tware’s Tjacket is a wearable touch technology vest that helps comfort people with ADHD, sensory disorders, and anxiety; and PlayMoolah conducts financial literacy and money management classes for kids to empowers next generation to handle money confidently.
    Singapore SEs also use their resources to aid developing countries, such as Wateroam, which developed affordable portable water filtration systems for communities lacking access to clean water.

  • TAIWAN

    With good multi-sector support driving agriculture- and crafts-oriented SEs, Taiwan’s social entrepreneurs are working toward a better, healthier future. Asher International Eco Health applies environmentally-friendly aquaculture techniques to farming fish to raise fish naturally; Earth Friend promotes clean organic products and educates farmers on the usage of natural products; Aurora Social Enterprise partnered with 50 organic aboriginal farmers to help them market their products; I Love Milk works to make dairy farming more ethical, efficient, and sustainable; and The Can works to preserve culture through crafts.

  • INDONESIA

    Sustainable agriculture, waste management concerns, and women empowerment has helped shape the landscape of SEs in Indonesia. Pandawa focuses on reducing the use of synthetic input (fertilizers and pesticides) in Indonesian agriculture; Nara Kreatif focuses on both social and environmental issues by providing housing and education to street children, as well as teaching them to upcycle waste into fashionable goods; Waste4Change promotes responsible waste management for a cleaner Indonesia; Salam Rancage employs women from low-income households to weave discarded newspaper into beautiful handicrafts, while Toraja Melo trains and protects the interests of women weaver artisans, and Du’Anyam offers sustainable employment to rural women as weavers in their home communities, helping reduce infant mortality rates for rural women who farm while pregnant due to poverty.

Be The Change,
Be The Catalyst.

As Asia continues to develop, social enterprises are crucial in providing solutions to social and environmental issues, and ensuring just, inclusive, and sustainable growth in the region. Social enterprises are setting the standards for the new way of doing business—proving to be viable, self-sustaining enterprises and creating social impact. SEs may still be in its infancy and face daunting challenges, but as one social entrepreneur said, “Be the change. be the catalyst.”
